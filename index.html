Final Project: Trends in Migrant Encounters and Border Seizures

Bernadette Fannin and Dahlia Mihalic

This project explores recent trends in migrant encounters and drug seizures across the U.S. border. Using interactive charts, we break down regional patterns, seasonal shifts, and enforcement outcomes across different states and sectors. Each section highlights a different part of the story- monthly encounter patterns, seizure activity, migrant origins, and the types of drugs being seized by state.

Monthly Encounters by AOR

Overview

This graph shows the number of monthly encounters by AOR (Area of Responsibility) over time. The x-axis represents the date, while the y-axis shows how many encounters occurred that month. Each colored line stands for a different AOR, making it easy to spot trends across regions.

Filter by State

There is a dropdown menu that lets you filter the chart by state. This makes it easier to focus on specific areas and see how they compare to others.

Tooltip Interactions

When you hover over the lines, a tooltip pops up showing details like the AOR name, date, and number of encounters for that point in time. It is a quick way to explore the data without losing the bigger picture.

Average Line

The chart also includes a dashed monthly average line. It acts as a reference point, so you can easily see which AORs are trending above or below the overall average.

Legend & Design

The scrollable legend shows all the AORs with matching colors. It's designed to be clean and uncluttered so you can focus on the data. Overall, the chart is simple to use, responsive, and informative.

Insight

The data shows that San Diego and Laredo are consistently among the highest in monthly encounters, especially during the spring and fall months. Tucson and Del Rio also show noticeable spikes mid-year. These trends help highlight how certain regions experience more activity depending on the season, and point to how migration patterns shift throughout the year.

Origin to Destination: Migration Flow Map

Overview

This map shows where migrants are coming from and where they're being encountered at the U.S. border. The arcs connect each country of citizenship to the U.S. sector where those encounters were reported.

Interactivity

Hovering over a country highlights the arcs showing where people from that country are arriving. The thicker the arc, the more encounters from that country to that destination.

Country Drill-down

Clicking a country reveals more details like who's arriving (demographic breakdowns) and which U.S. states they're arriving in most. These charts update automatically when you click.

Insight

Mexico, Guatemala, and Venezuela are some of the top origin countries, and most of their encounters are happening in Texas and Arizona. We also see rising numbers from places like Haiti and China, showing how the migration picture is expanding.

Encounters vs. Seizures by AOR

Overview

This graph is a grouped bar chart that compares the number of encounters and seizures by AOR (Area of Responsibility). The x-axis shows each AOR, while the y-axes show two different measures: the number of encounters and seizures on the left, and the total weight of seizures on the right.

Chart Breakdown

Each AOR has three bars: one for the number of encounters (the tall one), one for the number of seizures, and one for the weight of seizures. The bars are color-coded so you can easily tell the difference between the categories.

Tooltip Details

Hovering over any bar brings up a tooltip with extra info like the AOR name, number of encounters, seizures, and the total weight of seizures. This makes it easy to get the full picture without leaving the chart.

Extra Stats

The tooltip also shows stats like seizures per encounter and average weight per seizure. These are great for comparing how often seizures happen and how much is being carried when they do.

Insight

This chart shows that even though San Diego has a high number of encounters, Laredo stands out when it comes to average weight per seizure. It is a reminder that more activity doesn't always mean higher volume; smaller sectors can still lead the way in certain metrics.

Drug Quantities by State

Overview

This treemap shows the total weight of drugs seized by state. The size of each box represents the total quantity of drugs seized in that state between 2022 and 2024. The bigger the box, the more that state contributed to total seizures.

Drill-down View

Clicking on a state zooms in to show the breakdown by drug type. Each drug is also sized by how much was seized, giving you a closer look at what's showing up where.

Navigation

Clicking on any drug box will return you to the main view. This makes it easy to explore specific states and still quickly zoom back out to see the full picture.

Tooltip

A floating tooltip helps guide users and shows exact numbers when you hover. It's there to help make sense of the data at every step.

Insight

The treemap highlights how some states—like Texas and Arizona—lead not only in total seizures but also in specific drugs like fentanyl and methamphetamine. It's a helpful way to compare both volume and substance type across regions.
